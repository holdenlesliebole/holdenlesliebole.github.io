I am a PhD candidate in physical oceanography at Scripps Institution of Oceanography, UC San Diego, working with Mark Merrifield in the Coastal Processes Group. I am interested in environmental fluid dynamics, including wave-driven flows, boundary-layer dynamics, transport phenomena, and turbulence, as well as applications of the governing equations to measurements from marine field instrumentation.

My dissertation research focuses on the mechanics of cross-shore sediment transport, post-storm beach recovery, and wave–coastline interactions along the San Diego County coastline. I combine in situ hydrodynamic instrumentation, sonar and LiDAR surveys, satellite remote sensing, and analytical and numerical modeling to understand how sandy beaches evolve across scales, from individual storm events to multi-year morphodynamic cycles.

Before Scripps I earned my B.S. in Geology & Geophysics and Mechanical Engineering from Yale (2018), and spent several years working on international climate policy at The Carbon Institute. I'm broadly curious about applied coastal problems (erosion hazard, nourishment design, sea-level rise adaptation), and plan to sit for the California civil FE in summer 2026.

Research

My dissertation, On beach accretion, erosion, and morphodynamic change in Southern California and Northern Baja California, asks how waves and tides redistribute sand in nearshore beach systems across depth, across seasons, and across decades, and why observations so often outrun the existing models we use to predict them. Three principal chapters sit at the center of the work, plus a shorter collaborative chapter on regional sediment character.

Depth-dependent cross-shore sediment transport and post-storm beach recovery

Paper 1 · in preparation for JGR: Oceans

The 2022–2023 atmospheric river winter (17 landfalling ARs at the tail of a triple-dip La Niña) stripped Torrey Pines of its subaerial berm. The TBR23 field campaign (synchronous PUV arrays at nominal 5 and 7 m depth across two transects, jet-ski and vessel sonar, truck and ATV LiDAR) captured the recovery through summer 2023. Summer accretion turned out to be strongly depth-partitioned: onshore transport was confined shallower than about 4 m depth, while a large sand pad on the outer shoreface (4–8 m) remained essentially immobile under typical summer waves. I test how well Bailard energetics, wave-asymmetry terms, and an undertow correction reproduce this asymmetric cross-shore signature, and introduce a diagnostic cubic energy-flux proxy that captures it parsimoniously.

Profile modes, sandbar migration, and deep-offshore sediment motion

Paper 2 · in preparation

The 2022–2023 altimeter failure was not repeated: an 18-month cross-shore array at MOP586 (PUVs, altimeters, and echosounders at 5, 7, 10, and 15 m depth) caught the 2023–2024 El Niño winter and the recovery through 2025. I fold that record into the 1997–2025 Torrey Pines survey archive. EOF decomposition resolves the dominant cross-shore profile modes; wavelet and cross-wavelet analyses link them to wave forcing across event, seasonal, and interannual scales; the bar-scale altimeter records expose the migration and welding dynamics underneath. The question I want to answer: what minimum-complexity reduced-order model captures it all, across state transitions?

Decadal-scale morphodynamic variability across binational coastal sites

Paper 3 · in preparation, with MORDICS (UABC)

A 13-year record of topographic and bathymetric surveys across eight binational sites (Torrey Pines, Solana Beach, Cardiff, and La Jolla Shores in San Diego County; La Misión, Playa de Ensenada, Barro del Estero, and El Ciprés in Baja California) together with coincident wave hindcasts and sea-level anomalies. EOF modes of the decadal profile record are correlated against ENSO, PDO, and wave-climate indices to identify what persists across sites and what is genuinely local. A physics-informed generative diffusion model (work in collaboration) emulates the trained profile response and lets us run forward under counterfactual wave climates. This chapter situates the process-level work of Papers 1 and 2 within a regional, binational, decadal frame.

Alongshore distribution of beach sediment grain sizes across Southern California

Chapter 4 · collaborative appendix

A regional grain-size campaign spanning 120 km of coast from Dana Point south to Border Field, crossing three Southern California littoral cells. I'm collecting and analysing intertidal sediment at high alongshore resolution, fitting D3, D50, and D97 distributions to characterise not just median texture but the tails that sediment-transport formulations are most sensitive to. The alongshore patterns are compared against proximity to fluvial inputs, sandstone-cliff sources, canyon heads, and engineered transport barriers, with the output intended as a regional input layer for transport-model calibration. Short appendix chapter, joint with collaborators.

Field Work

Most of what I know about beaches comes from spending time on them with instruments. Field days are also when most of my better ideas show up.

The TBR23 campaign at Torrey Pines (winter 2022 through summer 2023) was the largest of these efforts: synchronous cross-shore PUV arrays at two transects and two depths for hydrodynamics, jet-ski and vessel sonar for subaqueous bathymetry, and truck and ATV LiDAR for the subaerial profile. The follow-on TBR23–24 deployment (late spring 2023 through summer 2025) placed working altimeters, echosounders, and PUVs on a single cross-shore array at MOP586 spanning 5, 7, 10, and 15 m depth, capturing the 2023–2024 El Niño winter and the recovery afterwards.

Smaller deployments and survey days fill in the rest. I'm out at Torrey Pines, Cardiff, and the cobble beaches of north county on a regular cadence, sometimes with the full survey kit and sometimes just with a tape measure and a notebook. In summer 2025 I led a 120-km intertidal grain-size sampling campaign from Dana Point south to Border Field (Chapter 4, above). I also occasionally help out other groups in the field; in 2023 I launched weather balloons for the Eastern Pacific Cloud Aerosol Precipitation Experiment.
